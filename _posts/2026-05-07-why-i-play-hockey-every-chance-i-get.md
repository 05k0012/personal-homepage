Like every good story, this one starts with a dog.

During the pandemic, everything really began. At the time, I was living in Bellevue and working at Microsoft, so being close to the Redmond campus mattered. My girlfriend then worked at Puget Sound Energy, which had a campus in downtown Bellevue. Naturally, we lived downtown. I loved it; we could walk everywhere, I took the bus to Microsoft, and she walked to work every day. It was actually great.

Once the pandemic hit, though, living in downtown Bellevue was not so great anymore. A lot of stores and restaurants closed, and neither of us needed to go into the office. That is when the idea of buying a house started to take shape. But houses in Seattle, Redmond, and Bellevue are extremely expensive.

I had spent a lot of time in Olympia and graduated from Evergreen, so I had plenty of friends there. I targeted a house in Olympia and ended up buying a really nice one, the kind of place I could not have gotten even a condo for in Bellevue. One of the reasons I wanted a house in the first place was because I have always loved dogs and always wanted one.

Oddly enough, I never thought boxers were cute or interesting-looking. I actually thought they were kind of ugly. But for one reason or another, there was a boxer named Grace at the animal shelter who needed a home, and she ended up being the dog. I remember that she loved to play ball, and they had trained her to fetch really well. She just seemed like a genuinely good dog, and she was available right when I was looking.

I had a yard, I had a house, and I felt like I needed a dog, so I pulled the trigger and got Grace.

Grace was obsessed with the ball. She could play fetch longer than I could throw. My arm would literally get tired. I got pretty good at throwing, but she always outlasted me. We played for hours some days, and I would take her to the dog park constantly.

A few adaptations came out of that. First, I started keeping a baseball bat in my car so I could hit long shots for her to chase. Second, instead of getting one of those Chuck It sticks, I ended up buying a hockey stick. She always dropped the ball right in front of me, so it was easy to smack it with the stick and let her chase it. That became the beginning of my obsession with the sport.

I spent countless hours in the backyard smacking a hockey ball around. For some reason, Grace preferred a street hockey ball over the bouncier Chuck It balls. A street hockey ball is sturdy, unlike a tennis ball, which she could destroy in a week, and she could never chew through it. So we played with that ball constantly, and I got pretty good at hitting it with the stick.

At one point, I decided to buy rollerblades to see how hard it would be to do all this while skating. I was extremely out of shape, so it was very hard. I hurt myself a lot learning how to skate.

As time went on, I became more curious about actually playing roller hockey. I started rollerblading around the neighborhood, and I would rollerblade with Grace to the park. We would take over a tennis court, and I would smack the ball around, trying to keep it away from her. She was so much fun. I really loved that dog.

And as it turns out, Olympia was one of the only places in the Puget Sound area with an active roller hockey scene. I stumbled onto it through a Facebook post and decided to go one day. I was hooked immediately.

I played my first game and was terrible. I could not stop. Luckily, the community was amazing. The guys were supportive. I even showed up wearing a bicycle helmet, and one guy gave me his old hockey helmet because I looked ridiculous. They encouraged me to keep coming back.

For someone like me, I think I have a little bit of focus issues, the game put me into a flow state. To play hockey, you have to track so many things: nine other people on wheels, all moving unpredictably, while you try not to run into them. At the same time, you are trying to move a rolling disc across a line into a tiny net while a guy in giant pants blocks most of the space.

Just surviving on the rink took all my mental processing. Every time I played, I was in a flow state. Then you get to the bench, exhausted, and the recovery feels incredible. I cannot describe it. It was like I found the thing I loved without even realizing I was looking for it.

I became a regular. That was the beginning of something that has now become a life obsession. I did not know it then, but I would become completely obsessed with hockey.

## From Roller Hockey to Ice Hockey

It did not take long before I wanted to try ice hockey. I was probably playing roller hockey in Olympia for about six months to a year before I realized I really wanted to give ice a shot. And just like with roller, I was instantly hooked.

I took what I thought was a learn-to-play or learn-to-skate class, but it turned out to be more of a skills class. They seemed to assume you already knew how to skate. I went out to Elevate Sports in Tacoma and bought my first pair of ice skates. I did not know much about them at the time, but little did I know the guy who helped me buy them would end up becoming a good buddy. I wound up seeing him all the time because I needed to get my skates sharpened there.

I bought the skates, and I do not even remember if I went to a public skating session first. I think I either jumped straight into what is called a stick and puck, which is basically an open ice session for people who want to practice hockey skills, or I went directly into the learn-to-skate class.

I remember taking the class and being terrible. I fell a lot and bruised myself up a lot. Nowhere in that class did they actually teach you how to ice skate. They just assumed you already knew how, and we mostly did hockey drills. It was a sink-or-swim situation.

After that first class, I felt like something was missing. I was not discouraged, but I knew I needed more. So I went on YouTube, started going to as many stick and pucks as possible, taught myself everything I could, and asked people for advice. That was huge for me.

Roller hockey was tons of fun, but if you have ever played both roller and ice, you know the difference. Ice hockey compared to roller hockey is like racing a go-kart versus racing an Indy car. The puck moves faster, you can move faster, and everything is bigger, faster, and more exciting. I was instantly hooked on ice hockey.

But there was one problem.

Olympia does not have an ice rink. The closest rink was Sprinker in Lacey, right next to Joint Base Lewis-McChord and not far from Tacoma. It was about a 40-minute drive. Anytime I wanted to play hockey, I had to commit to being in the car for about an hour and a half round trip. That is not something I enjoy.

I found myself wanting to play more and more. There was a drop-in game two nights a week at Sprinker, and I wanted to go to the Sunday stick and puck, and then I wanted to play even more on top of that. Driving out there two or three times a week really started to wear on me.

One thing I appreciated about those drop-in games was that the people I played with in Tacoma were much better players than me. Many were military guys from the base, or they had played hockey in high school or college. They accepted that I was bad, and I was fine being the worst person out there. The guy who sold me my first skates played those drop-ins too, and we would hang out in the parking lot afterward.

That was probably the first time I realized there was more to the game than just working out or being in a flow state. You could build a community around the sport. The people who played tended to be pretty cool, and I enjoyed their company. I made friends and some lifelong acquaintances just from learning to play in Tacoma at Sprinker.

But I also realized I was spending way too much time driving.

Then, unfortunately, my dog came down with a brain tumor, and she passed away. The specialist vet I took her to was close to the rink, and I worked through a lot of grief playing hockey. I remember one time I had to drop her off near the end, and I went over to play hockey while the vet was examining her. The sport helped me process everything.

After losing my dog, I found myself in a place where I was not ready to take on that journey again. I was living in a house far away from the place I was going to constantly for recreation, and I did not really need to be in Olympia anymore. I was playing a lot of hockey, both to deal with the grief and because I was obsessed with it. I was loving it.

So I looked at my situation and my finances and decided it made more sense to live next to a rink. I started looking at all the hockey rinks in the area and taking stock of what was available. I thought about living near Sprinker, but there was nothing around it. Sprinker is out in the middle of nowhere in Lacey, and many of the other rinks are not centrally located.

But there was one place: the Kraken Community Iceplex in Seattle. The location was amazing. It is next to Northgate Mall and very close to the Northgate light rail. I wanted to minimize driving, and there were apartments right next to the rink.

So I decided to move right next to the Kraken Community Iceplex. That began a chapter in my life of hardcore hockey playing. I was still working remotely doing security consulting, but now I could take lunch and go play hockey. If I wanted to walk across the street and play, I could. I could play to my heart's content.

## Building a Hockey Community in Seattle

When I moved across the street from the Kraken Community Iceplex in Northgate, everything changed. I started playing in the Kraken Adult Hockey League. A buddy of mine had joined a team and asked if I could play with them, and that is how I ended up on a team called the Roughnecks.

If you have ever seen the movie Starship Troopers, the name will make sense. The team was named after the protagonist’s military unit, Rico’s Roughnecks. The uniforms were designed in that futuristic sci-fi military style, borrowing iconography and even some of the spirit and phrases from the film. If you have not seen it, I highly recommend it. It is a silly, fun space fantasy movie.

This was the first time I had ever played organized hockey on a team with referees, periods, and everything that comes with real league play. It was a huge learning curve. I had to learn a lot about myself, about how to be on a team, and I had to ask myself the deeper philosophical question of why I was playing hockey in the first place.

During that first season, I was very competitive. I was not the friendliest person on the ice. I took a lot of penalties, and I was kind of a roughneck in the literal sense. I had to reflect on why I was there and why everyone else was there. At the end of the day, I was not there to win medals. I thought I needed that external validation of winning and being good at something, but even when we won medals and tournaments, it did not actually make me feel any better.

Sometimes I even felt shame if I hurt someone or played too rough, because that meant they did not have a good time. I started to realize that the best games were the ones where everybody, including my opponents, had fun. If I could help create that kind of game, I would have a good time too.

After games, both teams would often end up at the 32 Bar and Grill at the rink. If you could go up there, hang out, and share your passion for the sport with people you had just battled against, it was so much better than being a competitive person with a medal. That is when I started asking myself the deeper question: why am I playing hockey, and what do I want out of this?

As that evolved, I started playing on other teams. I became more of a member of the community and embraced the community aspect of the sport, not just the competitive nature of it. Competition is still important. People want a competitive game. Nobody wants you to just lay down for them. But it is more challenging to give people the right amount of competitiveness, to challenge everyone on the ice without overdoing it.

Playing to everyone’s level, creating a game where everyone gets a chance to be a hero, everyone gets a chance to touch the puck, and everyone comes off the ice feeling good is far more difficult than simply winning every game. I started asking how I could do that and why it mattered to me.

What came out of that reflection was simple. I enjoy the community. I enjoy the people I play with. I enjoy giving my team the opportunity to have fun while also giving the other team the chance to feel like they played a competitive and enjoyable game. I enjoy the camaraderie off the ice too. Sharing food and drinks with everyone at the rink became part of why I play.

Hockey gives you a workout whether you play aggressively or not, and that was another part of it. I wanted to burn calories, have fun, and make sure everyone else was having fun too. As a result, the enjoyment I get from the game has amplified exponentially. I probably play more than anyone I know, partly because I live so close to the rink, partly because I have the flexibility to play when I want, but mostly because I have the most fun when I play, and I try to make sure everyone else does too. That, to me, is the essence of great recreational hockey.

It has enriched my life in so many ways. My social circle has expanded immensely, and the quality of people I have met through the sport is incredible. I used to make friends through work, music, and other activities, but there is something about hockey that brings people together in a way nothing else does. I cannot imagine another activity that gets thirty people to drive all the way to Kent at ten at night on a Sunday. I could not get that many people to a barbecue, but to slide a disc around on ice, they will show up. That is beautiful.

Hockey has become both a social outlet and a passion for me. It is not an easy sport to get into. Skating is difficult, the equipment is expensive, and it takes dedication to get good. If it were not for the sport, I would not have met my girlfriend, who is an extremely high-level professional figure skater and coach. We both share a passion for skating. I love watching her skate. I am always slack-jawed when she jumps, spins three times in the air, and lands gracefully on a fraction of an inch of steel. I am terrified to even attempt that.

I have tried to get her into hockey. She will put on the pads and grab a stick, but once the puck gets involved, all that coordination has to be relearned. She does not share the passion for hockey, and that is fine. You have to want to play hockey to actually play it. I used to say everyone should play, but now I believe that if you want to, you should. It is one of the greatest experiences I have ever had, but it is not for everyone. You really do have to want it.

If you get over all the hurdles and learn how to play, the rewards are out of this world. It is one of the greatest things I have ever done, and I cannot imagine my life without the sport. It is my exercise, my social circle, my community. I love seeing the regulars at drop-ins, I love seeing my teammates, and I love building community through tournaments and events.

These are the reasons I play hockey every chance I get. I hope to play as long as I can, and I hope to play for the rest of my life. It truly is the greatest sport for me.
