Omelettes are one of my favorite ways to start the day. I think of it as a baseline cooking technique that I want to master and maintain. For me, making an omelette is as much about the process and consistency as it is about the flavor.

## My Morning Routine

I usually start by cracking three or four eggs into a bowl, adding some salt, and letting the mixture sit for a minute. The salt does something to the proteins in the eggs. It changes the texture, color, and richness, and also helps flavor the eggs themselves. While the eggs rest, I heat my pan to medium-low or between medium-low and medium heat.

Once the pan is heating, I make myself a cup of coffee on my girlfriend's Breville espresso machine. I usually make an Americano, but I also love the espresso options. Coffee and eggs together really set the tone for the morning.

## Preparing Ingredients

While my pan and coffee are ready, I like to get my fillings and toppings together. Cheese is essential for me. I usually use a high-quality aged cheddar. I try to source it from local farmers' markets whenever possible, but sometimes I use smoked cheese, goat cheese, or more exotic varieties. Gruyère is one of my favorites.

I also like garnishes that bring freshness and acidity. Pickled red onions, seasonal tomatoes, creamy yogurt-herb sauces, pears, or spicy Sriracha all work well. These help balance the richness of the eggs and cheese.

## Cooking the Omelette

I pour the whipped egg mixture into the preheated pan. I often use algae oil or olive oil, but sometimes I add a small knob of butter if I want extra richness. If I am adding meat like sausage or bacon, I cook it first and use the rendered fat to cook the eggs.

The technique I follow is inspired by Kenji Lopez-Alt. I let the eggs sit for a few seconds, then use a spatula to lift the cooked edges and tilt the pan so the uncooked eggs flow underneath. This creates a tender, slightly runny top while keeping a more set bottom. Once the eggs are mostly cooked, I take the pan off the heat and cover it with a plate for a minute or two. This traps the heat and finishes cooking the top while warming the plate.

Next, I add the cheese, cover briefly to let it melt, and then get ready to plate. I often fold the omelette into a half-clamshell or burrito shape, depending on how I am feeling that day.

## Toppings and Finishing Touches

Once plated, I add my garnishes. Pickled onions, fresh herbs, and a drizzle of olive oil are my usual go-to. Sometimes I add a few tomatoes if they are in season. Chopped green onions or chives are a classic topping. I also sometimes drizzle creamy yogurt-based ranch or Sriracha mayo for extra flavor.

If I am feeling indulgent, I will add a handful of arugula or other leafy greens inside the omelette for extra nutrition. Occasionally, I include a thin slice of prosciutto or other cured meat, paired with a drizzle of olive oil.

## Why Omelettes Matter to Me

For me, omelettes are more than just breakfast. They are high in protein, easy to customize, and a great vehicle for incorporating greens, cheese, and other flavors. The process itself is satisfying. It is simple but requires just enough technique that I feel accomplished, even on groggy mornings.

I often make this breakfast before heading to the farmers market or after early morning hockey games. It is my baseline dish, the one I come back to again and again. Even if it sometimes turns into scrambled eggs, that is fine too. It is a reliable start to the day and a perfect pairing with coffee.

The whole process takes about 10 to 15 minutes once you have streamlined it. It is quick, flexible, and deeply satisfying. It is a reminder that simple, well-executed cooking can be one of the best parts of the day.
